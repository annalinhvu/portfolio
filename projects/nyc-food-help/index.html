Resource guide

Learn the difference

Not sure what type of organization fits your situation? A plain-language guide by what you need.

🍲 I need a meal today
🍲
Soup Kitchen
A warm meal right now

Hot meals served on-site — usually breakfast, lunch, or dinner. No cooking or kitchen needed. Most open daily with no registration required.

Good if you need to eat today, no strings attached.
🏘️
Community Meal
Neighborhood-run meals

Hot food served on-site by churches, community centers, or neighborhood groups — usually on specific days. No ID or proof of need required.

Good if you want a community atmosphere alongside a meal.
🛒 I need groceries
🛒
Food Pantry
Take groceries home

Bags or boxes of groceries to take home and cook. Many use a "client-choice" model — you pick what your family will actually eat.

Good if you have a kitchen and want to cook for your household.
🚚
Mobile Pantry
Food comes to your neighborhood

Food trucks or pop-up markets bring groceries directly to neighborhoods. Schedules and locations rotate — check each org's website.

Good if you can't travel far, or there's no pantry nearby.
🏠 I cannot travel
🏠
Home Delivery
Food delivered to your door

Food brought directly to you if you can't leave due to illness, disability, or age. Requires a brief call to confirm eligibility.

Good for people with serious illness, disability, or mobility challenges.
👵
Senior Meals
For adults 60 and older

Congregate meals at community centers, or home delivery. Often paired with social activities and benefits counseling.

Good for adults 60+ who want meals and community connection.
🤝 I want to volunteer
♻️
Food Rescue
Redistributing surplus food

Food rescue groups collect surplus from restaurants, stores, and farms and redistribute it through partner pantries, shelters, community fridges, and meal programs.

Good to know: food rescue groups often supply partners — they may not always be direct walk-in sites.
🤝
Volunteer opportunities
How you can help

NYC food organizations need volunteers for sorting, serving, food rescue pickups, and delivery support. Many welcome first-timers with no experience needed.

View volunteer opportunities →

Why I built this

I started this after volunteering with food access organizations across NYC — soup vans, food rescue programs, pantries, and neighborhood groups. I kept seeing how much support exists, but how hard it can be to understand where to go and what to expect. This is a plain-language starting point, not a replacement for the official real-time tools.

⚠️ Hours change frequently. Always call ahead before visiting. This is a curated guide — for real-time listings use Food Bank For NYC or City Harvest, or call 311.

Need help by phone?

Call 311 and ask for emergency food assistance or food pantries.

For SNAP help, call NYC HRA at 718-557-1399.

For national help, call the USDA National Hunger Hotline at 1-866-3-HUNGRY.
